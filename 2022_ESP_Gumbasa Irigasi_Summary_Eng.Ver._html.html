🌾 Gumbasa Irrigation Rehabilitation & Reconstruction

Project Preparation Consultant (PPC) Final Report Summary | ADB Loan No. 3455-INO

English Version

📌 1. Background & Urgency

On September 28, 2018, a 7.4 magnitude earthquake struck Central Sulawesi, Indonesia, causing widespread destruction in Palu City and Sigi District. The Gumbasa Irrigation System, serving 8,148 hectares of farmland, was severely damaged — disrupting water supply to over 13,000 farming families and threatening food security and livelihoods. The Asian Development Bank (ADB) provided an Emergency Assistance for Rehabilitation and Reconstruction (EARR) loan. The consulting services began in October 2020, aiming to rebuild disaster-resilient infrastructure and restore agricultural productivity.

⚡ Urgency: Immediate restoration of irrigation for 8,148 ha, addressing earthquake, liquefaction, and flood risks while preventing further socioeconomic decline.

📋 2. Scope of Services

  • Post-disaster damage inventory and topographic mapping (12,000 ha).
  • Master Plan for earthquake & climate-resilient restoration.
  • Feasibility Study (FS) / Subproject Summary Report (SSR).
  • Detailed Engineering Design (DED) for main, secondary, tertiary canals and structures.
  • Environmental (IEE/AMDAL) and social safeguards (LARAP, IPP).
  • Bidding documents for 4 construction packages (total estimated IDR 961.5 billion).
  • Institutional recovery plan for Water User Associations (WUAs) and Irrigation Commission.

🎯 3. Objectives

  • Restore reliable irrigation to 8,148 ha of farmland.
  • Improve resilience to future earthquakes, liquefaction, and floods.
  • Modernize water management (SCADA, telemetry, decision support systems).
  • Enhance agricultural productivity, farmers' livelihoods, and food security.
  • Ensure environmental sustainability and social safeguards (involuntary resettlement, indigenous peoples).

🔧 4. Methodology

  • Field Surveys: Damage inventory, topographic (LiDAR), geotechnical (boreholes, CPT, SPT), environmental baseline (air, noise, water, soil, biodiversity).
  • Liquefaction Risk Assessment: Based on shear wave velocity, SPT, groundwater monitoring, and proximity to Palu-Koro fault.
  • Engineering Design: Indonesian KP standards with Build Back Better (BBB) principles — geomembrane linings, reinforced concrete structures, flexible joints, and stone columns.
  • Safeguard Compliance: ADB SPS 2009 — Category B for environment, involuntary resettlement, and indigenous peoples. Public consultations, grievance redress mechanism.
  • Economic Analysis: Cost-benefit analysis, EIRR (12.43%), BCR (1.33), sensitivity analysis.

📦 5. Key Outputs

  • Damage inventory reports & post-disaster maps.
  • Master Plan (including modernization, disaster management, agriculture market development).
  • Feasibility Study/SSR with economic viability.
  • Detailed Engineering Design for Packages 1A, 2, 3, 4 (weir, main canal, secondary, tertiary, drainage).
  • Bidding documents (ADB standard FIDIC-based).
  • Environmental IEE, EMP, LARAP, Indigenous Peoples Plan (IPP).
  • O&M Manual & long-term budgeting plan.
  • GIS maps, telemetry/SCADA design, hydro-mechanical gate specifications.

✅ 6. Outcomes

  • Technically feasible and economically viable reconstruction (EIRR 12.43% > 9% discount rate).
  • Command area confirmed at 8,148 ha (7,872 ha irrigable + 276 ha liquefaction-affected set aside).
  • Disaster-resilient designs: precast concrete + geomembrane lining, reinforced concrete structures, liquefaction countermeasures (stone columns, geotextile, drainage trenches).
  • Social safeguards: Category B resettlement (LARAP prepared) and Indigenous Peoples Plan for Ngata Pandehe and Waturalele groups.
  • Modernization roadmap: SCADA, telemetry, 22 monitoring stations, and remote gate operation.
  • Final workshop (Dec 20, 2021) and all deliverables approved by ADB and DGWR.

🌍 7. Impact

  • Economic: Restored agricultural production on 8,148 ha, increased farmer income, food security, and regional economy.
  • Social: Improved livelihoods for ~76,700 people, strengthened WUAs, reduced water inequity, and grievance redress mechanism.
  • Environmental: Reduced seepage, controlled sedimentation, avoidance of high-risk liquefaction zones for paddy, promotion of palawija (non-rice) crops on steep/porous soils.
  • Disaster resilience: Build Back Better (BBB) reduces future earthquake/liquefaction damage, protects infrastructure and communities.

♻️ 8. Sustainability

  • O&M Plan: Detailed O&M manual with budget allocation (1.5% annual routine, 7% periodic maintenance).
  • Institutional strengthening: WUA empowerment, capacity building, Irrigation Commission coordination, and telemetry-based water management.
  • Crop pattern shift: Limiting paddy to 72% of area, promoting palawija and drip irrigation on light soils to reduce liquefaction risk and improve water efficiency.
  • Environmental management: EMP, regular monitoring, and community participation through public consultations.
  • Funding: ADB loan ensures continued financial support; government APBN for O&M.

📘 9. Lessons Learned & Policy Recommendations

Lessons Learned:
  • Parallel implementation of Master Plan, FS, and DED (due to time pressure) complicated the process — the normal sequential order is more efficient.
  • Local subcontractors for geotechnical investigations lacked capacity, causing delays — complex projects need wider bidding (including Java).
  • Social and environmental safeguard staffing (TOR) was underestimated — actual requirements were more demanding.
  • Historical mistake (1970s) of allowing paddy on permeable/liquefaction-prone soils repeated; early soil studies ignored.
  • Delayed mobilization (2 years after earthquake) reduced time for proper planning.

📢 Recommended Policies for Future Projects:

  • Sequential planning: Always follow Master Plan → Feasibility Study → Detailed Design to avoid rework and complexity.
  • Rapid disaster response TA: Establish standby engineering teams (ADB/World Bank) to mobilize within 3 months post-disaster.
  • Zoning regulation: Enforce land use policy (local laws) prohibiting paddy cultivation on high-permeability/liquefaction zones; support palawija with drip irrigation.
  • Capacity building for local firms: For geotechnical and complex surveys, allow national (Java-based) bidders; invest in local knowledge transfer.
  • Adequate safeguard resources: Allocate longer duration and higher budget for environmental and social specialists, especially for Category B projects.
  • Monitoring & early warning: Real-time piezometric arrays around liquefaction zones (e.g., Petobo) to detect groundwater changes.
  • Integrated watershed management: Combine erosion control, sediment management, and reforestation to reduce canal siltation.

📄 10. Brief Policy Note

To the Government of Indonesia and ADB: The Gumbasa Irrigation Project demonstrates that post-disaster reconstruction must integrate land use zoning (restricting water-intensive rice on vulnerable soils), modernization (SCADA/telemetry), and community-based institutional strengthening. Future policies should mandate: (i) rapid mobilization of expert teams after disasters; (ii) strict adherence to sequential project preparation; (iii) flexible procurement to include experienced firms; and (iv) binding local regulations on cropping patterns in liquefaction-prone areas. The project's "Build Back Better" approach, if coupled with these policy shifts, can serve as a national model for climate- and disaster-resilient irrigation infrastructure.
