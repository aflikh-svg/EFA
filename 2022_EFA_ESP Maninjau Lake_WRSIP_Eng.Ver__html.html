Economic & Financial Analysis Summary
Maninjau Priority Lake, West Sumatera

Project: Preparation of Water Resources Strategic Implementation Plan for Priority Lakes – West Region (Toba–Maninjau)
Financing: Asian Development Bank (ADB) ESP & Government of Indonesia
Report Date: 2022  |  Evaluation Period: 2020–2039

1. Background & Urgency

Indonesia has designated 15 national priority lakes requiring integrated and sustainable water resources management. Lake Maninjau (West Sumatra) faces critical challenges: water pollution from floating net cages (KJA), sedimentation, reduced water quality, flooding, and declining biodiversity. The lake supports hydropower (64 MW), fisheries, agriculture, and tourism – but degradation threatens economic benefits and community livelihoods. The national RPJMN 2020–2024 prioritizes water security and ecosystem recovery, making urgent intervention necessary.

📌 Key urgency: Without intervention, annual economic losses from agriculture, fishponds, industries and buildings amount to IDR 300 billion in present value, plus irreversible environmental damage.

2. Scope

The project covers the entire Lake Maninjau catchment area (24,800 Ha) in Tanjung Raya District, Agam Regency. It includes five strategic sub-projects:

  • SP-1: Conservation (reforestation, bio-engineering, gully plugs)
  • SP-2: Utilization of water resources (groundsills, irrigation reliability)
  • SP-3: Damage control (river normalization, WWTP communal, flood protection)
  • SP-4: Water resources information system (early warning, climate monitoring)
  • SP-5: Community empowerment & supervision (capacity building, eco-tourism, green movement)

3. Objectives

Overall objective: Develop an integrated and sustainable water resources strategic implementation plan for Lake Maninjau to maintain environmental services and support economic sectors.

Specific objectives:

  • Enhance lake conservation and biodiversity protection
  • Improve water availability for drinking, irrigation and hydropower
  • Reduce flood damage to settlements, agriculture and industries
  • Increase tourism revenue through improved environmental quality
  • Strengthen institutional and community capacity for long-term lake governance

4. Methodology

Analysis follows ADB Guidelines for Economic Analysis (1997, 2014) and EFA spreadsheet models (ADB Indonesia Resident Mission, 2020).

  • Financial analysis: from beneficiary perspective (willingness to pay, investment, O&M costs, tourism revenues)
  • Economic analysis: With/without project comparison, shadow prices (SCF=0.99, unskilled labor CF=0.76), discount rate 9% (opportunity cost of capital)
  • Key indicators: ENPV, EIRR, EBCR, sensitivity tests (+10% cost, +10% damage, 1-year delay)
  • Damage assessment: Flood depth-damage curves (2- to 100-year return periods) for buildings, agriculture, fish cages, industries

6. Outcome (Expected improvements)

  • Reduction of annual flood damage: avoided losses of USD 2.04 million for buildings, USD 0.87 million for agriculture, USD 0.63 million for fish cages, and USD 0.12 million for industries.
  • Tourism revenue increases from IDR 764 B (without project) to IDR 2,077 B (with project) over evaluation period.
  • Improved water quality & reduced pollution from KJA (Keramba Jaring Apung).
  • Enhanced institutional capacity (local community & business groups).

7. Impact (Long-term & strategic)

The project contributes to Indonesia’s RPJMN 2020–2024 water resilience target, ADB Country Partnership Strategy (2020–2024), and SDG 6 (Clean Water & Sanitation), SDG 13 (Climate Action), SDG 15 (Life on Land). Long-term impacts include:

  • Reversal of lake eutrophication and securing of hydropower generation (64 MW)
  • Climate-resilient agriculture and flood risk reduction for 37,600 residents
  • Green tourism model (“Green Maninjau”) promoting sustainable local economy
🌍 Economic evaluation results: ENPV = IDR 65.36 billion | EIRR = 10.69% (above 9% discount rate) | EBCR = 2.16 ➜ Economically feasible.

8. Sustainability Strategy

Financial and institutional sustainability is ensured through:

  • Annual routine O&M budget (2.5% of civil works) and periodic O&M (7.5% every 5 years).
  • Community empowerment & supervision component (SP-5) including eco-farming, waste management, and tourism awareness.
  • Integration with local government (Nagari and District levels) and establishment of “Green Maninjau” movement.
  • Revenue streams from entrance tickets, parking fees, homestays, and tourism services.
  • Capacity building for 542 tourism business actors and 11,187 households.

9. Lesson Learning & Policy Recommendations

Key lessons:

  • Integrated lake management requires simultaneous action on conservation, water use, damage control, information systems, and community empowerment.
  • Tourism growth alone is insufficient without pollution control (KJA regulation, wastewater treatment).
  • Shadow prices and local willingness-to-pay data are critical for accurate economic viability.
  • Community-based monitoring (Green Maninjau) enhances ownership and long-term compliance.

Policy brief recommendations:

  • ➤ Strengthen cross-ministry coordination (PUPR, LHK, Tourism, Local Gov) for lake restoration.
  • ➤ Enforce carrying capacity limits for floating net cages and develop eco-friendly aquaculture.
  • ➤ Establish a dedicated Lake Management Authority with sustainable funding mechanism.
  • ➤ Scale up early warning systems and climate-adaptive infrastructure.
  • ➤ Replicate integrated planning approach to other 14 priority lakes in Indonesia.
