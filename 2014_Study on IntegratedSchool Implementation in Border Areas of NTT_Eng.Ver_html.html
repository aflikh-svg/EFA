🌏 Integrated School in Border Areas

Executive Summary | East Nusa Tenggara (NTT) Province, Indonesia

Final Report · 2014

📌 1. Background & Urgency

The NTT border region (directly adjacent to Timor-Leste) is strategically vital yet severely disadvantaged: low education levels, minimal infrastructure, and high socio-economic disparities. An Integrated School model (elementary–junior–senior high under one management, shared resources, seamless curriculum) is key to improving human capital, national resilience, and regional competitiveness. Geopolitically, the border represents Indonesia’s face to the world; quality education acts as a frontline defense for NKRI.

⚠️ Key fact: 5 characteristics of left-behind areas — poverty, unemployment, weak infrastructure, sectoral gaps, and low educational attainment.

🗺️ 2. Scope of Work

Target areas: 22 land & maritime border sub-districts in Kupang, TTU, Belu, Malaka, Alor, Rote Ndao, Malaka Barat, Kobalima Timur, etc. Activities include:

  • Coordination meetings & inventory of existing education facilities
  • Field visits, community dialogues (musyawarah/rembuk)
  • Population projection & school demand analysis
  • Formulation of concept design, recommendations, and periodic reporting

🎯 3. Objectives

To assess, formulate and recommend an Integrated School model for border areas in NTT, aiming to minimize educational disparities, reduce poverty, unemployment, and sustainably enhance human resource quality.

📊 4. Methodology

🔍 Projection analysis

Geometric population growth & school enrollment rates (APM) to forecast school-age children up to 2015, calculating classroom shortages.

⚖️ Potential & constraints

Identification of teacher shortages, electricity gaps, road access, while noting strong community participation and available land for expansion.

📍 Service location analysis

Standards: max distance (elementary 3 km, junior high 6 km), number of study groups, school capacity (2,000–6,000 inhabitants).

🏞️ Comparative analysis

Cross-sub-district comparison of geography, demography, social conditions, availability of TK/SD/SMP/SMA, and support infrastructure (water, electricity, roads).

Implementation period: 4 calendar months (preparation → literature review → field surveys → data processing → final reporting).

📄 5. Outputs

  • Comprehensive inventory of educational facilities across 22 border sub-districts.
  • Analysis of integrated school applicability based on factual data & projections.
  • Conceptual model of integrated school (one-roof management, curriculum articulation, resource sharing).
  • Technical recommendations for priority locations & minimum infrastructure standards (classrooms, labs, library, dormitories).

📈 6. Outcomes

  • Establishment of 3 recommended integrated school clusters in cross-sub-district border zones (Nanaet Duabesi–Kobalima Timur; Lamaknen Selatan–Lamaknen; Bikomi Nilulat–Miomaffo Barat).
  • Enhanced access to junior & senior high education for areas previously lacking SMA (e.g., Lamaknen Selatan, Nanaet Duabesi, Kobalima Timur).
  • Design standards for earthquake-resistant, eco-friendly buildings with student dormitories to overcome extreme travel distances & rainy season disruptions.

🌟 7. Impact (Long-term)

👥 Social

Reduced dropout rates, increased education awareness, strengthened nationalism in vulnerable zones.

📈 Economic

Improved human capital → local workforce competitiveness, potential poverty & unemployment reduction.

🏛️ Geopolitical

Border as “Indonesia’s showcase” with national-international standard schools, reinforcing NKRI sovereignty.

🌿 Environmental

Integrated schools with sanitation, clean water, and green spatial planning serve as eco-friendly models.

♻️ 8. Sustainability

The integrated school model is designed to be managed through synergy among Central Government (BNPP), Local Governments, and community participation (unified school committee). Sustainability provisions include:

  • Dedicated border-area budget allocation (DAK + provincial APBD) for routine maintenance.
  • Engagement of private sector & international organizations for ICT and language labs.
  • Multi-competency teacher training & incentive schemes for permanent placement in border areas.
  • Phased construction: priority on junior-senior high clusters, then dormitories & support facilities.

🧠 9. Lesson Learning & Policy Recommendations

✓ Key lesson: Border education cannot be fragmented; an inter-sub-district cluster approach is essential due to difficult geography. Dormitories, clean water, and solar power are critical success factors.

📌 Policy recommendations:

  • Accelerate RTRW legalization in Belu, TTU, Kupang, Malaka to provide legal certainty for integrated school sites.
  • Promote “Boarding Integrated Schools” with minimum land & room standards based on study groups (technical annex).
  • Integrate DAK Physical & Non-physical funding for school construction + teacher training + scholarships for border poor students.
  • Build cross-border cooperation with Timor-Leste in cultural exchange & border-relevant curricula to strengthen Indonesian identity.
  • Establish a Provincial Integrated School Management Unit to oversee resource sharing (teachers, labs, sports facilities).

📑 10. Brief Policy

Proposed policy title: “Accelerated Construction of Boarding Integrated Schools in NTT Border Areas using a Sub-district Cluster Model”.

Proposing institutions: Ministry of Education, National Border Management Agency (BNPP), NTT Provincial Government.
Indicative budget: Priority for 3 cluster locations (estimated cost for junior & senior high + dormitories + supporting facilities).
Performance indicators: 40% reduction in senior high dropout rates within 5 years, increased gross enrollment rate (APK), and 100% accredited A/B integrated schools within 6 years.

⭐ Core recommendation: Policy must integrate road/bridge infrastructure and electricity as a comprehensive border area package — not isolated school construction.
