Final Report Summary | IDN0224

Child Marriage Prevention Project – Indonesia (Bogor)

Reporting period: September 2014 – August 2015 | Funding: ANO | Implementing partner: Plan Indonesia & RutgersWPF

1. Background & Urgency

Child marriage remains a critical issue in Indonesia, especially in Bogor District, West Java. Under the Indonesian Marriage Law No. 1/1974, the minimum marriage age for girls is only 16 (boys 18), and religious courts often grant dispensations, particularly in cases of teenage pregnancy. Unregistered marriages are common, making accurate data scarce. The project was initiated to address high rates of child marriage, its negative impacts on girls’ education, health (maternal mortality, reproductive damage), and perpetuation of poverty. The urgency was reinforced by a lack of comprehensive Adolescent Sexual and Reproductive Health (ASRH) modules and limited government funding for youth peer education on child marriage prevention.

2. Scope

Location: Bogor District, West Java Province, Indonesia.
Duration: Originally 3 years (Sept 2014 – Aug 2017), but due to budget cuts reduced to 1 year (Sept 2014 – Aug 2015).
Target groups: Adolescents (in/out of school, including street children and marginalized youth), Bogor Youth Forum members, parents, government officers (BPPKB), and community cadres.
Main implementing partners: Plan International Indonesia, RutgersWPF (ASRH expert), Bogor District Government (Women Empowerment & Family Planning Agency – BPPKB), Bogor Youth Forum, and the Centre for Gender and Child Studies – IPB University.

3. Objectives

Following project modification due to budget cuts and Ministry of Home Affairs (MoHA) permit delays, the revised objectives were:

  • Outcome 1: Girls and boys are empowered to protect themselves from child marriage, early pregnancy, and gender-based violence through increased knowledge, youth participation, and awareness campaigns.
  • Outcome 2 (partial integration): Parents and community members become aware of child marriage negative impacts and contribute to prevention (via module development for parents).
  • Outcome 4: Civil society and coalitions influence government policies and public support (national coalition establishment).

Specifically, the project aimed to develop two evidence-based prevention modules (for youth facilitators and parents) and strengthen the capacity of Bogor Youth Forum as peer educators.

4. Methodology

The project adopted a participatory and human‑rights based approach (CCCD principles – Children at the Centre, Inclusion, Gender Equality). Key methods included:

  • Participatory training & ToT: Child protection and gender‑transformative workshops using “Stick and Stone”, “Bamboo Shoot”, and CCCD modules.
  • Multi‑stakeholder coordination: Regular meetings with Bogor district government (BPPKB, Bappeda, Education, Health), religious leaders, and civil society.
  • Module co‑creation: Consultative workshops, focus group discussions (FGDs), and piloting of ASRH‑integrated modules with youth and parents, followed by pre‑test/post‑test evaluation.
  • Network building: Establishment of the “Stop Child Marriage National Coalition” (Dec 2014) and a local multi‑sectoral network facilitated by IPB University.
  • Monitoring & evaluation: Pre/post tests, observation, FGDs, in‑depth interviews, and field follow‑up of action plans.

5. Key Outputs

  • 5.1 Capacity strengthening:
    • 1 ToT for 21 Bogor Youth Forum mentors/facilitators (child protection, gender, child marriage prevention).
    • 2 participatory trainings for 76 Bogor Youth Forum members (committee & general members, including PIKR and shelter youth).
    • 8 youth facilitators (4 girls, 4 boys) certified and deployed.
  • 5.2 Modules developed:
    • Child Marriage Prevention Module for Youth Facilitators (ASRH + child protection + gender + life skills).
    • Child Marriage Prevention Module for Parents (parenting, communication, adolescent development, preventing child marriage).
    • Both modules piloted (23‑25 July 2015 with youth; 13‑15 August 2015 with 27 parents/cadres) and finalised.
  • 5.3 Coalitions & networking: National coalition “Stop Child Marriage” launched (Dec 2014) involving Ministry of Women Empowerment, religious organisations (NU, Muhammadiyah, MUI, etc.), and civil society. Local workshop (April 2015) with 46 participants from local NGOs, universities, and government.
  • 5.4 Awareness actions: Youth facilitators conducted 9+ awareness events reaching ~3,760 indirect beneficiaries (school orientations, community gatherings, national family planning day, children’s day, etc.).

6. Outcomes Achieved

  • Increased knowledge & attitude change: Post‑test results showed significant improvement: understanding of child protection increased by 57%, child rights by 115%, and gender concepts by 40‑65%. Participants changed from blaming girls’ clothing to recognizing systemic responsibility and referral mechanisms.
  • Youth agency & leadership: Youth forum members developed and implemented action plans independently (e.g., creating ICT materials, leading sessions at school orientation, advocating at community level). Two girls were elected as vice president and treasurer of Bogor student body (OSIS) following gender awareness sessions.
  • Parental engagement: Pilot training for parents (BPPKB cadres) generated commitment to socialise child marriage prevention in their sub‑districts. Parents recognised the interconnection of gender, reproductive health, and child protection.
  • Multi‑stakeholder coordination: Bogor government (BPPKB) acknowledged the modules and requested continued support. A functional referral pathway for violence (friends → teachers → P2TP2A → police) was mapped by youth participants.

7. Impact (Preliminary & Potential)

Although the project duration was shortened, early impact evidence includes: (i) direct rejection of child marriage by trained youth mentors and their active peer education; (ii) strengthened local institutional capacity – Bogor Government now has two ready‑to‑use modules (youth & parents) to integrate into their limited‑budget programmes; (iii) shift in individual attitudes: e.g., a participant from an Islamic background stated “This training changed my perspective on patriarchy and how it fuels child marriage”. At the community level, awareness reached marginalised groups (street children, shelter youth) and fostered non‑discrimination towards LGBT youth. The national coalition created a platform for sustained advocacy on raising the minimum age of marriage to 18.

8. Sustainability

  • Institutional anchoring: The modules are owned by Plan Indonesia and RutgersWPF, but they were co‑designed with BPPKB Bogor and can be used by any government or civil society organisation. BPPKB has already integrated the modules into their youth peer educator programme.
  • Youth network continuity: Bogor Youth Forum continues to exist with government facilitation. The 8 trained facilitators remain active and have trained new members. The “Stop Child Marriage” national coalition continues advocacy work (though needing further funding).
  • Knowledge products: Two comprehensive, context‑sensitive modules (available in hardcopy) ensure future trainings can be replicated without external technical assistance.
  • Recommendation for scaling: With modest funding, the modules could be disseminated to other districts in West Java and nationally, using Plan’s existing networks.

9. Lessons Learned & Policy Recommendations

Lessons learned

  • Bureaucratic delays (MoHA permit) significantly hindered community‑level work. Future projects should secure all provincial/district permits before budget commitment.
  • Short‑term funding (3 years → 1 year) forced a shift from community mobilisation to module development and capacity strengthening, which proved effective but limited direct outreach. Investing in high‑quality, adaptable modules yields lasting value.
  • Gender and religious sensitivity must be addressed carefully. Participatory methods that link local values with gender equality can reduce resistance (e.g., using “tree analysis” instead of abstract gender theories).
  • Youth‑led awareness is highly effective when young people are given ownership, training, and follow‑up support. The peer‑to‑peer model created ripple effects.
  • Inclusive facilitation works: sessions that integrated street children and LGBT youth required active trust‑building, but ultimately reduced prejudice and fostered empathy.

Policy Recommendations

  • Harmonise marriage law with child protection law: Increase minimum age of marriage for girls from 16 to 18 without exception. Encourage district religious courts to apply stricter scrutiny on dispensation requests.
  • Allocate district budget for ASRH and child marriage prevention: Bogor government should formalise the use of the two modules and provide annual funds for peer educator programs and parent‑school awareness campaigns.
  • Simplify NGO working permits at district level: Establish a fast‑track mechanism for child protection projects to avoid implementation paralysis.
  • Mandate birth registration and marriage registration: Strengthen civil registration to prevent age falsification and unregistered child marriages.
  • Integrate gender and child protection into school curricula: Use the modules developed as supplementary resources in formal and non‑formal education, especially in Islamic boarding schools (pesantren).

10. Brief Policy Brief

Title: Preventing Child Marriage through Participatory Modules and Youth Empowerment – Lessons from Bogor
Key message: Child marriage persists in Indonesia due to legal loopholes (minimum age 16), low awareness, and lack of practical resources. The IDN0224 project demonstrated that even within 6 months of active implementation, targeted ToT and ASRH‑based modules for youth and parents generate significant knowledge shifts and community action.
Recommendations for policymakers (national & district): (1) Revise Marriage Law to set minimum marriage age at 18 for both sexes. (2) Adopt and distribute the “Child Marriage Prevention Module for Youth Facilitators and Parents” across all Puskesmas, schools, and P2TP2A units. (3) Allocate specific budget line for child marriage prevention in Bogor’s APBD and replicate BPPKB’s partnership model with civil society. (4) Train religious court judges on the long‑term consequences of granting dispensations. (5) Strengthen data collection on child marriage through integrated civil registration and health information systems.
Target audience: Ministry of Women Empowerment and Child Protection, BAPPENAS, Provincial Government of West Java, District Government of Bogor, and parliamentary commissions on health and gender equality.
