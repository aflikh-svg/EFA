Summary Report

Child Marriage Prevention Module for Adolescent Facilitators

Final Draft – Comprehensive Training Material

1. Background and Urgency

Child marriage remains a critical issue in Indonesia, where the legal minimum marriage age for girls is 16 (compared to 19 for boys), and many girls marry even younger due to social, economic, and cultural pressures. Indonesia ranks 37th globally for child marriage prevalence and second in ASEAN. This practice violates children's rights, reinforces poverty cycles, and exposes adolescents — especially girls — to severe reproductive health risks, violence, and educational dropout. The module was developed to empower adolescent facilitators to lead peer discussions, fill knowledge gaps on sexual and reproductive health and rights (SRHR), gender equality, and child protection, and ultimately prevent child marriage at the grassroots level.

2. Scope

The module targets adolescents aged 13–18 years, both male and female, including those still in school, dropouts, or active in community settings. It is designed for peer facilitators who have prior experience or interest in gender, child protection, and reproductive health. The content covers: gender and sexuality, power relations, violence, child protection, sexual and reproductive health, puberty, unwanted pregnancy, technology risks, and future planning. It is adaptable for mixed or single-sex groups (up to 25 participants) and can be used in schools, communities, or training centers.

3. Objectives

  • General objective: Equip adolescent facilitators with knowledge and skills to prevent child marriage through peer education.
  • Specific objectives:
    • Understand SRHR and gender equality correctly.
    • Recognize reasons why child marriage must be stopped.
    • Make informed decisions to prevent unintended pregnancy.
    • Collaborate with parents, families, and communities to support healthy life choices.

4. Methodology

The module employs participatory and experiential learning methods, including: pre/post-tests, icebreakers, games (e.g., ball toss, body mapping, “Who and Where Am I”), metaplan card exercises, role plays, case study discussions, video screenings (“Impossible Dream” and a documentary on child marriage), group work, problem tree analysis, solution tree, campaigns, and action planning. Sessions are facilitator-led but highly interactive, ensuring safe, respectful, and inclusive environments. Total training time ranges from approximately 12–16 hours, delivered over several days or weekly sessions.

5. Outputs

📘 Training Module

Complete facilitator guide including 7 chapters, session plans, handouts, case studies, and visual aids.

📝 Assessment Tools

Pre-test and post-test questionnaires with 20 statements to measure knowledge change.

🎭 Educational Media

Body maps, metaplan cards, film screenings, role-play scenarios, and posters for campaigns.

📋 Action Plans

Participant-developed action plans (6 months) for community peer education and advocacy.

6. Outcomes

  • Increased knowledge among adolescents about gender equality, sexual health, and legal aspects of child marriage.
  • Improved ability to identify and reject risky sexual behaviors and avoid unintended pregnancies.
  • Strengthened communication skills to discuss future plans and negotiate with parents/guardians.
  • Enhanced awareness of child protection mechanisms and referral pathways for violence cases.
  • Reduced stigma and discrimination towards sexual and gender diversity (LGBTQ+).
  • Adolescents empowered to become agents of change in their communities through peer-led campaigns.

7. Impact

  • Short-term: Improved SRHR knowledge, safer behaviors, and increased self-efficacy among participants.
  • Medium-term: Reduction in child marriage cases in targeted communities; increased school retention for girls; lower rates of adolescent pregnancy.
  • Long-term: Breaking the intergenerational poverty cycle; healthier, better-educated future generations; stronger gender-equitable norms.
  • Systemic: Greater community demand for enforcement of minimum marriage age (18) and improved child protection policies.

8. Sustainability

Sustainability is embedded through:

  • Training of adolescent facilitators who then train peers (multiplier effect).
  • Action plans and monitoring & evaluation frameworks designed for local implementation without external support.
  • Integration into school and community-based youth groups.
  • Use of low-cost, locally available materials (metaplan cards, plano paper, balls, etc.).
  • Collaboration with existing child protection mechanisms (P2TP2A, teachers, religious leaders).
  • Emphasis on family and community dialogue to sustain behavior change.

9. Lessons Learned and Policy Recommendations

📌 Key Lessons

  • Peer education is highly effective for sensitive topics like SRHR and child marriage.
  • Gender-transformative approaches (challenging power relations) are essential, not just informational.
  • Separate sessions for boys and girls may be needed in conservative settings, but mixed groups foster mutual understanding.
  • Involving parents and community leaders increases acceptance and reduces resistance.
  • Current legal loopholes (minimum marriage age 16 for girls) undermine prevention efforts.

📢 Policy Recommendations

  • Raise minimum marriage age to 18 for both sexes without exceptions (amend UU No. 1/1974).
  • Mandate comprehensive sexuality education in formal and non-formal curricula.
  • Strengthen child protection systems at village level and train officials on gender-based violence.
  • Fund peer education programs as a cost-effective strategy for adolescent health and rights.
  • Align all laws (marriage, child protection, population administration) to a uniform definition of "child" as under 18.
  • Promote economic empowerment for girls and families to reduce financial drivers of child marriage.

10. Brief Policy Summary

This module provides evidence-based, participatory tools for adolescent facilitators to prevent child marriage. Its implementation supports Indonesia’s commitments to SDG 5 (gender equality), SDG 3 (good health), and the Convention on the Rights of the Child. To maximize impact, policy makers should: (1) harmonize national laws to set 18 as minimum marriage age; (2) integrate SRHR and gender equality into school curricula; (3) invest in community-based peer education networks; and (4) establish monitoring mechanisms for child marriage cases. The module’s success demonstrates that adolescents are capable leaders in social change when equipped with appropriate knowledge and skills.
