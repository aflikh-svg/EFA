📊 TelDok Business Plan Summary Report

Strategic Telemedicine Platform (Based on 2017 Plan) | Evidence-Based Policy Brief

🔍 1. Background & Urgency

Indonesia faces critical healthcare disparities: unequal distribution of medical personnel, limited access to specialists, high consultation costs, inefficient referral systems, and geographical archipelago challenges. Patients lack instant access to trusted medical advice; junior doctors struggle to build loyal patient bases. BPJS incentives remain low relative to medical risks, while technology infrastructure for health is fragmented. The 2017 plan identified 172,000 doctors with severe maldistribution (Jakarta-centric). Telemedicine becomes urgent to bridge gaps, reduce costs, and improve emergency response.

Key urgency factors: 326 million mobile subscribers, but no integrated tele-consultation platform; unmet need for remote, paid physician advice; rising middle class demands faster, quality healthcare access without physical visits.

📌 2. Scope

TelDok platform encompasses 8 integrated digital services: Med Consult (paid video/voice), Med Hospital (referral system), Med Visit (house calls), Med Info (health articles), Med Ambulance, Med Food (healthy meals), Med Prescription, and Med Lab. Phase I focuses on teleconsultation (Java & Bali, target women 18–45 y.o., health budget IDR 150k/month). Phase II–IV adds hospital, lab, pharmacy, ambulance, and telemedicine ecosystem. Geographic coverage starts in major cities then expands nationally.

🎯 3. Objectives

  • Increase healthcare accessibility through a rapid, accurate tech platform (TelDok).
  • Enable compensated virtual consultations for doctors (currently unpaid phone advice).
  • Create loyal patient-physician relationships via digital tools, especially for junior doctors.
  • Develop a complete health ecosystem: consultation → prescription → lab → food → ambulance.
  • Recruit 6,000 doctors (out of 172k) nationally, with senior doctors mentoring network.
  • Launch telemedicine by end 2018 and become “Top Leader” in Indonesian health-tech.

⚙️ 4. Methodology

Strategic approaches: SWOT analysis (strength: large doctor pool & single pricing; weakness: funding gaps & absent regulations; opportunity: 172k doctors currently unpaid for phone consults; threat: doctor burnout & competition).

Recruitment model: Doctor referral, senior doctors (earn Rp.50/min from network), medical representatives (commission), social media (professional orgs), hospital partnerships (Pelni, Hermina), health events.

Promotion: Offline (business cards, posters, women’s organizations, portable clinics) and online (SEO, Google AdWords, social media campaigns, email/SMS blasts).

IT infrastructure: Collaboration with XL Axiata, Telkom, UNESA, UGM for voice and app reliability; call center based in Yogyakarta (lower UMR) staffed by nurse-diploma agents, 24/7 shifts.

Phased development roadmap from Med Consult to full telemedicine by Phase IV.

📦 5. Output

  • Fully functional TelDok mobile app (Android/iOS) with premium consultation, hospital referral, e-prescription, lab ordering, ambulance GPS tracking, and healthy food delivery.
  • Network of 6,000+ physicians + senior doctors structure + hospital partnerships (RS Pelni, Hermina, Kimia Farma, Prodia, Pramita Lab).
  • Operational 24/7 call center (Yogyakarta) handling inbound medical triage and support.
  • Training modules and referral system for junior doctors to retain patients.
  • Telemedicine apparatus readiness by 2018, along with promotional materials (posters, e-banners, doctor business cards).

📈 6. Outcome

  • Doctors receive fair compensation (premium per minute), expand patient base beyond geographic limits, reduce idle time.
  • Patients get 24/7 instant access to trusted doctors, avoid unnecessary travel and waiting, lower out-of-pocket costs (single pricing strategy).
  • Partner hospitals/labs/pharmacies experience increased referrals and operational efficiency.
  • Reduced inequality: Java & Bali initial target, but remote areas gain from teleconsultation when connectivity exists.
  • Improved continuity of care via digital prescriptions and lab results directly to physicians.

🌟 7. Impact

Health system impact: Decreased burden on overstretched hospitals; rationalized specialist referrals; improved management of chronic diseases through remote follow-ups; faster emergency response (Med Ambulance). Economic impact: new income streams for doctors, creation of health-tech jobs, cost savings for patients (reduce travel & missed work). Social impact: empowerment of women (primary decision-makers), improved health literacy via Med Info, and gradual decongestion of urban healthcare facilities. Over 3–5 years, could improve Indonesia’s health equity index, especially in underserved islands.

♻️ 8. Sustainability

Financial sustainability: Multiple revenue streams: premium calls (non-insurance & insurance), SMS/video, hospital/lab/pharmacy revenue sharing, Med Food margins, telemedicine apparatus, in-app advertising. Monetization designed to be self-sustaining after scale-up.

Operational sustainability: Senior doctors incentive model (Rp.50/min from their network) ensures continuous recruitment and mentoring. Corporate user groups & loyalty schemes for patients. Partnerships with Grab/Uber for logistics and Kimia Farma/Prodia guarantee service continuity.

Technology sustainability: Regular updates & maintenance, GPS integration, scalable call center operations.

📚 9. Lesson Learning & Policy Recommendations

Key lessons from TelDok (2017 plan): (1) Telemedicine thrives when doctors are adequately compensated and supported by senior mentorship. (2) Uneven regulations across provinces hinder platform scalability — central guideline needed. (3) Offline promotional channels combined with digital recruitment deliver higher doctor adoption. (4) Call center with medical background improves trust. (5) Single pricing strategy simplifies user decisions.

Policy recommendations:

  • Establish a national telemedicine regulatory framework (licensing, data privacy, cross-provider recognition).
  • Include tele-consultation fees in BPJS or other social security schemes for selected chronic diseases.
  • Provide subsidies or tax incentives for startups building health-tech infrastructure in rural areas.
  • Mandate digital health records interoperability and invest in internet connectivity for remote regions (village satellite/4G).
  • Support continuous medical education for doctors on telemedicine ethics and digital tools.

📋 10. Brief Policy

Policy title: “National Strategy for Integrated Telemedicine and Digital Health Equity”

Problem statement: Unequal access, underutilized doctor workforce, and lack of legal clarity for telemedicine prevent equitable healthcare in Indonesia.

Proposed action: (a) Enact Telemedicine Act defining responsibilities, reimbursement, and cross-provincial licensing. (b) Create a central digital health authority to accredit platforms like TelDok. (c) Allocate universal service obligation funds for connectivity in 3T regions (frontier, outermost, remote). (d) Pilot performance-based incentives for doctors using telemedicine in underserved areas. (e) Encourage public-private partnerships (PPP) for ambulance GPS tracking, e-pharmacy, and lab integration.

Expected result: 30% reduction in unmet medical needs in rural areas within 4 years; increased doctor productivity; lower household out-of-pocket spending through transparent pricing.
