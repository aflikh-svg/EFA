📊 Infrastructure Financing Optimization Study

Banking & Non-Bank Financial Institutions for Public Works and Spatial Planning | Final Summary Report

1. Background & Urgency

Adequate infrastructure is a cornerstone of economic growth. Studies (Calderon & Serven, 2004; IMF 2014) show that every US$1 invested in infrastructure generates a US$3 multiplier effect. Indonesia’s infrastructure quality ranks 92nd out of 144 countries (WEF 2013), lagging behind other Asian nations and even South Africa.

The government faces a massive funding gap: Bappenas estimates that IDR 5,519 trillion is needed for infrastructure in 2015-2019, but state budget (APBN) can only cover about IDR 2,215 trillion. Private participation via PPP/KPS has not yet shown a success story. Hence, alternative financing – including banking, insurance, pension funds, PT SMI, and PIP – must be optimized.

2. Scope

Regional coverage: Five provinces – West Java, DKI Jakarta, Central Java, East Java, and Bali.
Activity scope: Review of laws/regulations; data collection from banks and other financial institutions; evaluation of potentials and constraints; workshops and FGDs; formulation of policy recommendations to accelerate infrastructure development in the Public Works (PU) sector.

3. Objectives

  • Analyze potentials and constraints of banking & other financial institutions in financing PU infrastructure.
  • Formulate investment policy recommendations to increase the role of these institutions in PU development.

Targets: Identify current roles; evaluate investment potential/barriers; produce a policy recommendation book and database.

4. Methodology

📚 Literature & Regulation Review

Analysis of 24+ legal instruments (UU Perbankan, UU Keuangan Negara, Perpres KPS, etc.) plus international case studies (Korea, India, Thailand).

🏦 Surveys & Field Studies

Interviews with major banks (Mandiri, BNI, BCA), non-bank institutions (PT SMI), and provincial offices (East Java, Central Java) covering water, roads, sanitation, housing.

🔄 FGD & Stakeholder Workshops

Focus Group Discussion involving Ministry of Finance, OJK, BPJS, contractors' associations, and academics to validate findings.

5. Outputs

  • Database: Investment profile of banking and other financial institutions in PU infrastructure (aggregated without violating bank secrecy rules).
  • Policy Recommendation Book: Comprehensive strategies for leveraging pension funds, insurance, PT SMI, and IIF.
  • Policy Paper: Concise executive brief for decision-makers.

6. Outcomes

  • Clear identification of barriers: long break-even period, maturity mismatch between short-term deposits and long-term infrastructure loans, lack of collateral, political/social risks, and bank secrecy restrictions (PBI No. 2/19/PBI/2000).
  • Recognition of large untapped potential: pension funds (BPJS ~Rp198 trillion by Oct 2015) and insurance assets can be directed to infrastructure via new instruments.
  • Stakeholder alignment: consensus on the need to consolidate PIP and PT SMI into a stronger infrastructure bank (LPPI).

7. Impact

Successful implementation of the recommendations can:

  • Reduce the infrastructure funding gap by mobilizing Rp1.192 trillion from banking and non-bank sources.
  • Improve Indonesia’s infrastructure competitiveness and support economic growth (GDP multiplier effect).
  • Enhance project bankability through design-build-lease (DBL) schemes and credit enhancement via the Indonesia Infrastructure Guarantee Fund (IIGF).
  • Accelerate road, water, and housing projects, directly improving public welfare and reducing regional disparities.

8. Sustainability

The study proposes institutional and regulatory reforms that ensure long-term financing continuity:

  • Legal amendments: Revise pension and insurance investment regulations to allow direct infrastructure participation.
  • Institutional strengthening: Merge PT SMI and PIP into the Indonesian Development Financing Institution (LPPI) with stronger capital and mandate.
  • Pipeline development: Continuous coordination between Ministry of PUPR, Bappenas, and financial institutions to prepare bankable projects.
  • Scalable models: DBL and performance-based annuity schemes can be replicated across regions.

9. Lessons Learned for Policy Recommendations

  • Korea’s success stems from strong political leadership, dedicated special accounts (Road Sector Account), and alignment with 5‑year economic plans.
  • India demonstrates the effectiveness of Viability Gap Funding (VGF), standardized PPP documents, and state-level PPP units.
  • Thailand highlights the need for clear PPP supervision committees and handling of unsolicited projects.
  • Indonesia-specific constraints: Banks face strict BMPK limits and lack of unconditional guarantees; contractors struggle with the 3:1 debt-to-equity ratio (DER) requirement (now eased to 4:1 by PMK 169/2015, but special treatment for infrastructure is needed).
  • Bank secrecy (PBI 2/19/2000) hampers data collection – policy should allow aggregated reporting for national development planning without violating privacy.

10. Brief Policy Summary & Recommendations

🏛️ Institutional Consolidation

Merge PIP and PT SMI into a single strong infrastructure bank (LPPI) or transfer PIP’s assets to SMI to boost capital and operational efficiency. Accelerate regulation by Ministry of Finance & DPR.

💰 Unlocking Pension & Insurance Funds

Revise UU 11/1992 and related rules to allow pension funds (BPJS) to invest directly in long-term infrastructure projects through PT SMI or IIF, using listed/unlisted instruments.

🏗️ Design-Build-Lease (DBL) Scheme

Government funds initial construction (via international loans or bonds) and later transfers operations to private sector once cash flows stabilize. Use performance-based annuity payments.

📜 Regulatory Adjustments

Provide special DER exemptions for infrastructure contractors; streamline land acquisition funding through APBN; and enhance unconditional bank guarantees to speed up project disbursement.

🎯 Key immediate actions: Form a coordination team (Kemenkeu, OJK, PUPR, Bappenas) to define project pipeline; finalize merger of SMI & PIP by 2017; issue implementing regulations for pension fund investment in infrastructure.
