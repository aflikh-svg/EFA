📊 Summary Report: Digoel Dam Project
Economic & Financial Feasibility Analysis

Project: Consulting Services for Development of Project Preparation Consultant (PPC) for Digoel Dam, Papua Province
Report Reference: FDL - PROJECT ECONOMIC SUPPORTING REPORT (rev 12-07-2022)
Currency Basis: IDR (March 2022) | Exchange Rate: USD 1 = IDR 14,451

1. Background & Urgency

The Digoel Dam in Papua Province is a strategic infrastructure project designed to support regional development through multi-sector benefits. Papua and West Papua provinces face challenges in food security, energy access (95.75% electrification ratio, with PLN supplying only 58.25%), and clean water availability. The dam aims to address these gaps by providing irrigation for 6,090 hectares of agricultural land, generating 26.20 GWh/year of hydroelectric power, and supplying 274,000 m³/day of raw water. The urgency is driven by the need to reduce dependence on subsidies, enhance economic productivity, and support sustainable development in eastern Indonesia.

2. Scope

The report covers the full economic and financial evaluation of the Digoel Dam construction project, including:

  • Cost estimation based on detailed engineering design (DED) for all major components: main dam, diversion tunnel, spillway, intake structures, hydromechanical & electrical works, access roads, and facility buildings.
  • Financial analysis (using market prices including taxes and subsidies).
  • Economic analysis (adjusting for shadow prices, removing transfer payments, applying Standard Conversion Factor of 0.85, and valuing unskilled labor at 76% of financial wage).
  • Sensitivity analysis for critical variables (±10% to ±20% changes in costs and benefits).
  • Benefit assessment for agriculture irrigation, hydropower (PLTA), and raw water supply.

3. Objectives

  • To determine the Financial Internal Rate of Return (FIRR) and Financial Net Present Value (FNPV) to assess project viability from an investor’s perspective.
  • To determine the Economic Internal Rate of Return (EIRR), Economic Net Present Value (ENPV), and Economic Benefit-Cost Ratio (E-BCR) from a national economic perspective.
  • To perform sensitivity analysis to identify key risk factors and project resilience to cost overruns or benefit shortfalls.
  • To provide policy recommendations for financing arrangements and project implementation.

4. Methodology

The analysis follows international guidelines (ADB standards) and Indonesian government regulations. Key methodological elements:

  • Cost estimation: Unit prices based on Boven Digoel Regent Decree 2021, equipment productivity analysis, contractor overhead & profit (15%), and taxes (VAT, import duties).
  • Economic adjustment: Removal of transfer payments (taxes, subsidies, contractor profits); application of Standard Conversion Factor (SCF = 0.85) for non-traded goods; unskilled labor shadow price = 76% of financial wage.
  • Discount rate: Economic opportunity cost of capital at 9% (ADB threshold for energy/agriculture/transport projects). Financial discount rate based on BI 7-Day Repo Rate (6% as reference).
  • Project life: 50 years economic life, with 3 years preparation + 4 years construction (total 7 years before operation).
  • Sensitivity scenarios: Cost increase (10%, 20%), benefit decrease (10%, 20%), and combined variations.

6. Outcome

  • ✅ Economic Feasibility: EIRR of 9.13% exceeds the 9% ADB threshold, making the project economically viable.
  • ⚠️ Financial Feasibility: FIRR of 8.26% is below typical commercial expectations but above the 6% opportunity cost of capital, indicating marginal viability requiring public funding or subsidies.
  • 📈 Sensitivity Analysis: Under combined 20% cost increase + 20% benefit decrease, EIRR drops to ~7.2% (still above 6% but below 9%). The project is moderately sensitive to delays and price changes.
  • 🌾 Agricultural Benefit: IDR 60.77 billion/year economic benefit from 6,090 ha irrigation, with double-cropping potential.
  • ⚡ Hydropower Benefit: 26.20 GWh/year, economic value IDR 45.16 billion/year.
  • 💧 Raw Water Benefit: 274,000 m³/day, economic value IDR 3.05 billion (NPV).

7. Impact

  • Economic: Improved food security, reduced rice imports, job creation during construction (unskilled labor from local households), and increased regional GDP.
  • Social: Electrification of remote villages (PLN target of 1,123 villages in Papua & West Papua), better access to clean water for domestic use, and reduced poverty through agricultural income growth.
  • Environmental: Hydropower displaces diesel generators, reducing CO₂ emissions. However, land acquisition and resettlement require careful management (costs included as separate estimates).
  • Fiscal: The project requires government subsidies to achieve financial viability (FIRR 8.26% < commercial rate). Economic benefits justify public investment.

8. Sustainability

The project includes provisions for operation & maintenance (O&M) costs over 50 years. Sustainability measures include:

  • Local community involvement in O&M through training programs (implied by use of local unskilled labor).
  • Hydropower revenue (IDR 45.16 billion/year economic) can be partly reinvested in dam maintenance.
  • Water allocation prioritizes agriculture and domestic use, with flexibility for climate variability.
  • Institutional framework: Ministry of Public Works (PUPR), local government, and PLN/PDAM as off-takers.

9. Lessons Learned & Policy Recommendations

📌 Key Lessons

  • Economic viability (EIRR 9.13%) is stronger than financial viability (FIRR 8.26%) due to tax/subsidy distortions — highlighting the need for public financing.
  • Unskilled labor shadow price at 76% of market wage significantly reduces economic costs; similar adjustment should be applied to other infrastructure projects in rural areas.
  • Hydropower benefits are more stable than agricultural benefits (which depend on weather and market prices).
  • Land acquisition and resettlement costs, though separate from engineering estimates, are critical for project success and must be budgeted early.

📋 Policy Recommendations

  • Blended finance: Combine government budget (APBN) with soft loans from development banks (ADB) to cover the financial gap.
  • Tariff adjustments: Gradually increase electricity and water tariffs to improve FIRR while protecting poor households through targeted subsidies.
  • Risk mitigation: Include contingency of at least 15% for construction costs given Papua's remote logistics and potential delays.
  • Community engagement: Formalize local labor training and hiring quotas to maximize economic multiplier effects.
  • Monitoring framework: Establish a post-project evaluation system to compare actual EIRR/FIRR with projections, especially for agriculture yield and hydropower generation.

10. Brief Policy Summary

The Digoel Dam is economically viable (EIRR 9.13% > 9% threshold) and generates substantial national benefits in irrigation, hydropower, and water supply. However, financial returns (FIRR 8.26%) fall below commercial expectations, necessitating public subsidies or concessional financing. The project aligns with Indonesia's goals for food security, renewable energy, and equitable development in Papua. Key policy actions: approve public funding, secure ADB co-financing, implement local labor provisions, and establish a long-term tariff roadmap. Without government support, the project will not attract private capital, but with it, the socioeconomic returns justify implementation.
