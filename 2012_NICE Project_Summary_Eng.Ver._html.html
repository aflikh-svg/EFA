NICE Project – Independent M&E

Final Summary Report | TA-4963-INO (September 2012)

Nutrition Improvement through Community Empowerment

📍 1. Background & Urgency

The Government of Indonesia and ADB signed a loan/grant for the NICE Project in 2007, targeting 1,800 poor villages across 6 provinces (NTT, NTB, North Sumatra, South Sumatra, South Sulawesi, West Kalimantan). The final objective was to improve nutrition services for vulnerable groups (pregnant/lactating women and children under five).

Urgency: High stunting and underweight prevalence, low exclusive breastfeeding, and weak community-based health services. The project introduced Community Nutrition Groups (CNCs/KGM) that received block grants (CNP/PGM) to address local nutrition issues. All project disbursements had to be completed by September 2012, making the final M&E critical.

📐 2. Scope of TA Activities

  • Strengthening M&E systems across all levels (village to central).
  • Developing Community-Based Nutrition Monitoring System (CBNMS).
  • Monitoring financial compliance and transparency of CNCs.
  • Field evaluation in 24 sample CNCs (2 per province covering districts/cities).
  • Review of data flow, reporting formats, and capacity building for facilitators & financial staff.

🎯 3. Objectives

General

Assess CNC performance, compliance with financial rules, transparency, and overall contribution to reducing malnutrition.

Specific

Validate data reliability, empower communities, document best practices and provide actionable policy recommendations for sustainability and replication.

🧭 4. Methodology

Mixed-method evaluation: document reviews, in-depth interviews (CNC chairs, treasurers, members, village heads, health workers, beneficiaries), and financial transaction verification. 24 CNCs selected as representative sample across six provinces. Field visits between May–August 2012. Quantitative logframe indicators (D/S ratio, coverage of Vitamin A, Fe supplementation, exclusive breastfeeding) were tracked from 2010–2012, complemented by qualitative assessment of transparency and institutional capacity.

Sample coverage: 1 district + 1 municipality per province → 2 CNCs per district → total 24 CNCs + additional problematic CNC cases.

📦 5. Key Outputs

  • Standardized financial & progress reporting forms (Form 3.5–3.11) rolled out from CNC to CPMU.
  • CBNMS prototype designed and piloted in Jeneponto (South Sulawesi) – though not fully implemented due to perceived community burden, valuable lessons for future community surveillance.
  • Training materials and hands-on sessions for financial compliance, data validation, and proposal development.
  • Transparency boards installed in most CNCs, although public awareness varied.
  • Consolidated national & provincial reports on CNP fund utilization: cumulative disbursement IDR 183.8 billion, realized IDR 138.8 billion (as of Aug 2012).

Key outcomes: stronger community empowerment, improved posyandu (integrated health posts) attendance, increased knowledge of healthy food for children and pregnant women, reduction in diarrhea & ARI cases according to puskesmas reports. D/S (attendance rate) reached >75% in many districts by 2012. Maternal and child nutrition counseling and "mothers’ classes" became routine. Financial management and bookkeeping at CNC level improved, though data entry remained partly manual.

🌱 7. Impact

Positive shift in behavioral change: 90% of beneficiaries acknowledged tangible improvements (reduced child sickness, better nutritional knowledge, cleaner school environments). Innovative activities (fish ponds, duck farming, catering, “jimpitan” savings) began generating local revenue for posyandus. The project strengthened cross-sector collaboration between health offices, education, and community leaders. The formation of CNCs became a sustainable asset for puskesmas and villages, enabling better targeting of undernourished children and follow-up home visits.

♻️ 8. Sustainability & Replication

Villages and puskesmas committed to using ADD (village funds) and BOK (health operational funds) to continue key activities: cooking demonstrations, sweeping (home visits), supplementary feeding for malnourished children. Replication already planned: Makassar allocated budget for 79 new urban villages; Lombok districts rolled out replication in 2–6 villages each; OKI district planned “Mandiri KGM” (independent CNCs). Exit strategy includes transforming innovative activities into small businesses (jeruk orchards, fish ponds, catering) and establishing cooperation agreements between CNCs and local government/private donors.

Recommendation: formalize partnership agreements, strengthen CNC capacity in proposal writing to attract CSR/donors, and ensure transparent financial reporting to maintain public trust.

📚 9. Lessons Learned & Policy Recommendations

  • Proposal & disbursement delays: Long bureaucratic processes and lack of binding timelines hindered implementation. Recommendation: set clear processing deadlines and decentralize proposal appraisal to district/capacity-building teams.
  • Training gap: Many CNCs lacked practical training in financial management and innovative planning. Provide continuous “on the job” training and simplified, visual guidelines.
  • Data validity issues: Inconsistent reporting formats across puskesmas and villages. Standardize forms, digitize data entry at puskesmas with offline validation, and keep original copies at village level.
  • Transparency: Only 10% of community members knew about financial transparency boards. Actively disseminate through meetings, village announcements, and involve village heads.
  • Innovation absorption: Replication requires local ownership; integrate CNCs into village medium-term development plans (RPJMDes).

📋 10. Brief Policy Recommendations

For Ministry of Health & Local Gov

  • Adopt CNC model as a national community nutrition cadre under Puskesmas.
  • Allocate recurring BOK/DAK funds for home visits, food demonstrations, and growth monitoring.
  • Simplify proposal formats with clear ceilings for each activity type.

Financial & M&E Integration

  • Integrate SIGIZI with community-level data (posyandu & CNC) for real-time surveillance.
  • Mandate quarterly transparency reporting displayed in public spaces.
  • Link CNC innovation funds with village-owned enterprises (BUMDes).

Final verdict: NICE Project demonstrated that community block grants combined with facilitation and structured M&E significantly improve nutrition behaviors and posyandu performance. To scale impact, policy must prioritize capacity building, simplified administrative procedures, and long-term funding integration into routine health budgets.
