NICE Project – Gender Perspective Report

Women’s Empowerment, Participation, and Social Transformation in Community Nutrition

🔍 Gender-responsive analysis | TA-4963-INO

⚖️ 1. Why a Gender Lens on NICE?

The Nutrition Improvement through Community Empowerment (NICE) Project operated in 1,800 villages across 6 Indonesian provinces, targeting pregnant women, lactating mothers, and under-five children. While the project was not formally designed as a “gender project,” its entire architecture — Community Nutrition Groups (CNCs/KGM), block grants (PGM), and Posyandu activities — heavily relied on women’s participation, leadership, and unpaid care work. This report dissects the gendered outcomes, challenges, and transformative potential based on final M&E data (Sept 2012).

📌 Key gender principle: NICE shifted from viewing women only as beneficiaries to positioning them as decision-makers, treasurers, community organizers, and innovators. 81% of CNC members were women, yet structural barriers remained.

In almost all CNCs, women held key positions: treasurers, secretaries, and many served as chairpersons. However, male involvement was minimal (only about 10% of participants in mother’s classes were men). Village heads (mostly male) supervised CNC accounts alongside female treasurers — a dual-gender accountability mechanism. Despite strong representation, political interference and community distrust led to CNC dissolutions in some areas (e.g., Botot village, North Sumatra), often disproportionately affecting women’s leadership continuity.

✨ Insight: Women’s double burden (domestic work + CNC duties) was not explicitly compensated, yet they reported high motivation due to visible improvements in children’s health.

💰 3. Financial Control & Economic Empowerment

CNC bank accounts required joint signatures (chairperson + treasurer) and village head approval — a system that prevented elite capture. Women treasurers were trained on basic bookkeeping, transparency boards, and reporting. Key financial data as of August 2012:

  • IDR 138.8 billion total realized funds managed at CNC level, with women controlling day-to-day expenditures for nutrition interventions.
  • 46% of funds spent on improving posyandu coverage and maternal/child activities (mother’s classes, cooking demos, supplementary feeding).
  • Innovative businesses (duck farming, fish ponds, catering, gas reselling) — 60% initiated and run by women — generated income for posyandu sustainability.
📊 Gender & transparency: Although 88% of CNCs displayed financial transparency boards, only 10% of community women interviewed were aware of the amounts. Recommendation: targeted oral dissemination to overcome literacy barriers.

🧕 4. Unpaid Care Work & Time Poverty

Field findings revealed that women faced significant time constraints: collecting water, farming, fishing, and caring for children often conflicted with posyandu schedules. In coastal NTT, women spent hours fetching water or fishing, making it difficult to attend regular health posts.

Challenges identified

  • Posyandu held during peak farming/ fishing hours → low attendance
  • Home visits and “sweeping” by female cadres added extra unpaid hours
  • Many women reported skipping nutrition sessions due to domestic workload

Project adaptations

  • Extra weighing days before/after posyandu day
  • Home visits for those who couldn't come
  • Joint cooking demonstrations that reduced individual prep time

CNCs used PGM funds to provide snacks and incentives (small gifts, competitions) — which partly compensated for women’s time. However, no systematic remuneration for cadres existed. The project inadvertently relied on women’s altruism, but it also enhanced their social capital and bargaining power within households.

Exclusive breastfeeding remained a challenge due to mixed messages from some health workers (recommending formula). Nevertheless, mother’s classes and nutrition counseling directly strengthened women’s agency in deciding infant feeding. Moreover, the project increased iron/folate supplementation coverage for pregnant women and Vitamin A for postpartum women.

📢 Gender-responsive success: Husbands were rarely involved, but when they participated in community meetings, nutritional outcomes improved faster. Policy implication: engage men as allies in future nutrition programs.

🚫 6. Barriers: Social Norms & Institutional Gaps

  • Low male engagement: Only 10% of “mother’s class” participants were men; household decisions often made without paternal support.
  • Political interference: In some villages (Tapanuli Tengah), male-dominated local politics dismantled functional CNCs, interrupting women-led initiatives.
  • Literacy & digital divide: 48% of CNCs submitted handwritten reports; women cadres rarely had computer training, reinforcing manual, time-consuming processes.
  • Lack of gender-disaggregated data: Logframe indicators tracked D/S, Vit A, Fe, but not differential participation rates by sex of household head or caregiver burden.
⚠️ Critical gap: No separate analysis of how many female-headed households benefited, nor how women’s control over PGM funds affected intra-household food allocation. Future projects should adopt gender-sensitive M&E indicators.

🌟 7. Transformative Practices: Women's Innovation Groups

Several CNCs demonstrated strong economic agency by launching small businesses funded partially by PGM innovation grants:

  • Duck farming (NTB, CNC Dasan Cermen) & fishponds (OKI, CNC Terusan Menang) – income pooled for posyandu sustainability.
  • Orange orchards (Pangkep, South Sulawesi) – women managed harvesting and sales.
  • “Jimpitan” (voluntary savings) in Makassar – small weekly contributions from mothers to create a post-project fund.
  • Gas reselling & catering services – women-led micro-enterprises that funded nutrition classes.

These activities shifted women from pure volunteers to economic actors. However, the report notes that written cooperation agreements were rarely used, exposing women to potential conflict. Recommendation: formalize profit-sharing and management roles to protect women’s interests.

♻️ 8. Sustainability: Will Women’s Gains Last?

Exit strategy discussions revealed that most puskesmas and village governments intend to continue CNC activities using BOK (health operational funds) and village ADD budgets. However, sustainability is gendered:

Positive signals

  • Replication budgets (Makassar for 79 new kelurahan) explicitly allocate funds for CNC formation.
  • Women cadres express willingness to continue without immediate pay, due to social recognition.
  • “Mandiri KGM” (OKI) to formalize women’s economic groups.

Risks

  • Unpaid care work may lead to burnout without incentives.
  • If male village heads reallocate CNC funds, women’s control could erode.
  • Lack of gender budgeting in routine APBD may sideline women-specific activities (PMT, classes).

📚 9. Lessons Learned & Gender Policy Recommendations

  • Integrate gender-transformative indicators: measure women’s decision-making power, time use, and access to project resources.
  • Compensate care work: provide small honorariums or food incentives for female cadres to acknowledge their workload.
  • Targeted male engagement: mandatory father/paternal involvement in at least one nutrition session per quarter.
  • Simplify financial transparency tools: use pictorial transparency boards and verbal announcements to reach women with low literacy.
  • Replicate women-led innovation funds: allocate 10–15% of block grants to small enterprises with clear gender-equal profit-sharing agreements.
  • Establish gender focal points at DPIU/PPCU level to monitor participation barriers and sexual/gender-based violence risks (none reported but should be proactively addressed).
💡 Strategic shift: From “women as participants” to “women as managers of nutrition security” → ensure local regulations (Perdes) recognize KGM as a formal women-led community institution.

🏁 10. Conclusion: Advancing Gender Equality through Nutrition Governance

The NICE Project inadvertently became a powerful vehicle for women’s empowerment in rural Indonesia. Women gained skills in financial management, public speaking, collective action, and micro-enterprise. The project reduced malnutrition while also shifting household nutrition decisions toward mothers. However, without explicit gender mainstreaming tools, some inequalities persisted — especially unpaid labour and limited male involvement.

Final recommendation for ADB & Government of Indonesia: Future nutrition projects should deliberately adopt a Gender Action Plan with dedicated budgets, female facilitator quotas, time-use surveys, and gender-disaggregated M&E. The CNC model offers a blueprint: combine block grants with local ownership, then layer on gender-transformative elements to achieve both nutrition and women’s empowerment outcomes.
