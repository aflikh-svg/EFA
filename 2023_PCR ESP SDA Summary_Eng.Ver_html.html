Accelerating Infrastructure Delivery Through Better Engineering Services Project (ESP)

Project Completion Report (PCR) Summary | Directorate General of Water Resources (DGWR) | Loan No. L3455-INO

Implementation Period: Dec 2016 – Apr 2023 | ADB Loan: USD 80.66 million (revised)

1. Background & Urgency

Indonesia's Ministry of Public Works and Housing (MPWH) faced significant constraints in delivering infrastructure targets due to complex land acquisition, procurement delays, and inadequate project preparation. To accelerate infrastructure delivery, the Asian Development Bank (ADB) provided a loan for the Engineering Services Project (ESP) aimed at improving the quality and timeliness of public investment programs in the water resources sector. The project supported DGWR's Strategic Development Plan (2015–2019 extended to 2020–2024) and addressed critical gaps in feasibility studies, detailed engineering designs, environmental safeguards, and land acquisition planning.

Urgency: Without improved project preparation, infrastructure projects would continue to face delays, cost overruns, and poor sustainability, impacting food security, water supply, and flood protection for millions.

2. Scope

The ESP covered 26 contract packages across five directorates under DGWR, including 9 irrigation packages, 4 river basin flood management packages, 5 groundwater & raw water packages, 7 dams & lakes packages, and 1 project management consultant package. Activities included preparation of master plans, feasibility studies (FS), detailed engineering designs (DED), environmental impact assessments (EIA/AMDAL), land acquisition and resettlement plans (LARP), and bidding documents for civil works.

3. Objectives

  • Output 1: Improve quality and timely start-up of infrastructure projects (irrigation, flood management, raw water, dams, and lakes).
  • Output 2: Strengthen DGWR's capacity in public investment management, including master planning, design standards, value engineering, asset management, and safeguards.
  • Outcome: Strengthened capacity of DGWR to efficiently design and implement MPWH's public investment program.
  • Impact: Attainment of infrastructure objectives under DGWR's Strategic Development Plan (SDP) 2020–2024.

4. Methodology

The project utilized a two-stage consultant selection method: (1) establishment of a panel of consulting firms (empanelment) through pre-qualification, and (2) call-down assignments for specific projects. Advanced technologies such as LiDAR, SCADA, SIPASI (Irrigation Management System), and QLARP (digital LARP tool) were deployed. The Project Management Office (PMO) and four Project Implementation Units (PIUs) coordinated implementation, supported by a Project Management Consultant (PMC). Capacity building was delivered via workshops, webinars, and technical clinics.

5. Key Outputs

  • 26 contracts awarded (25 PPC packages + 1 PMC) with total value IDR 1,148 billion (~USD 79.6 million).
  • Completed feasibility studies, DEDs, and bidding documents for 9 irrigation systems (e.g., Way Sekampung, Wadaslintang, Mrican, Saddang, Gumbasa, Asahan Phase 1).
  • Flood management master plans and DEDs for 4 river basins (Seluna, Cimanuk-Cisanggarung, Belawan, Mahakam).
  • Raw water supply master plan (2020-2040) covering 164 regencies/cities and 40 regional SPAM developments.
  • Dam and lake projects: Jragung Dam (completed), plus Digoel, Rongkong, Surumana, Warsamson dams, and strategic plans for priority lakes (Toba, Maninjau, Tondano, Mahakam Cascade).
  • Institutional strengthening: 9 major training/workshop events (Value Engineering, SIDLACOM, Irrigation Modernization, Flood Risk Management, LARP, etc.).
  • Technical guidelines developed for Provisional Sums, economic-financial analysis, LARP, environmental reports, O&M of rivers/coasts, dam FS/DED, lake FS/DED, and water resources management plans.

6. Outcome

By project completion (April 2023), cumulative disbursement reached USD 62.93 million (78% of revised DGWR allocation). The project successfully prepared 26 infrastructure sub-projects, catalyzing an estimated USD 10.49 billion in future public investment. Capacity building significantly improved DGWR staff competencies in procurement, safeguards, financial management, and contract administration according to ADB guidelines. However, delays occurred due to COVID-19, complex procurement (first-time empanelment system), and extended approval processes.

7. Impact

The ESP is expected to contribute to GDP an estimated USD 6.99 billion during 2019–2023 from facilitated public investments. The benefit-cost ratio (BCR) is estimated at 132.21 based on facilitated public investment. Sector impacts include increased cropping intensity (up to 300% in some areas), reduced flood damage, improved raw water security, and enhanced dam safety. Multiplier effects ranged from 56x (dams & lakes) to 136x (raw water supply).

8. Sustainability

Downstream financing has been planned for 41 projects across APBN, ADB, World Bank, and PPP schemes. Operational and maintenance (O&M) sustainability depends on effective participation of Water User Associations (WUAs), tourism awareness groups, and local governments. The project fostered ownership among farmers and beneficiaries, but risks remain regarding local government budget allocation for O&M and transfer of water costs to WUAs. Overall, the project is judged likely sustainable.

9. Lessons Learned & Policy Recommendations

  • Complex contract packaging: Combining multiple stages (survey, MP, FS, DED, safeguards) into one PPC contract proved ineffective. Future projects should separate these into sequential contracts.
  • Panel system improvement: The empanelment system reduced prequalification time, but panel members were insufficient for the number of packages, leading to quality dilution. Add more panel members for future ADB loans.
  • Coordination intensity: Regular coordination between PMO, PIUs, PMC, and PPCs (2-3 times/month) improved quality. This model should be replicated.
  • Provisional sums contracts: Sub-consultant contracts lacked clarity on mobilization, output quality, and monitoring. Standardized clauses are needed.
  • Financial management: Front-load DIPA revisions and standardize report review timelines to avoid end-of-year disbursement pile-ups.
  • COVID-19 resilience: Future projects should include contingency mechanisms for remote work, foreign expert entry permits, and flexible survey methods.
  • Safeguards integration: Environmental and social documents (AMDAL, IEE, LARP) require early preparation and dedicated PPC experts with full-time availability.

10. Brief Policy Recommendations

  • Sequential contracting: Break down large project preparation into stage-wise contracts (survey → MP → FS → DED → safeguards).
  • Strengthen panel system: Expand panel size and enforce quality assurance mechanisms.
  • Improve provisional sums governance: Mandate clear sub-consultant performance indicators and PMC oversight.
  • Accelerate approval processes: Set KPI-based time limits for DIPA revisions and technical report reviews by Tim Direksi.
  • Enhance O&M sustainability: Require local governments to commit O&M budgets before civil works completion, and strengthen WUA financial capacity.
  • Mainstream digital tools: Continue use of LiDAR, SCADA, SIPASI, and QLARP, but ensure adequate training and post-project handover.

Overall project assessment: Successful (Highly Relevant, Effective, Efficient, Likely Sustainable).
